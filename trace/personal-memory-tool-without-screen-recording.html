The problem

Why screen recording is the wrong approach

The idea of "remember everything you did" sounds great. The problem is that most tools that attempt it do so by recording continuous video of your screen — and that comes with consequences that rarely appear on marketing pages.

Privacy. Your screen captures visible passwords, private messages, financial documents, client data — everything. Even with local encryption, you're storing a permanent record of information you never intended to archive.

Performance. Recording video 24/7 consumes constant CPU and RAM. Screenpipe and similar tools are known to slow down machines and drain laptop batteries. That's not an implementation bug — it's the inherent cost of continuously capturing pixels.

Work use. Most companies have security policies that would prohibit a screen-recording app running in the background on a work machine. The tool becomes a compliance problem before you even install it.

There's a better approach: capture only the semantic signals that matter, not the pixels. That's what Trace does.

The approach

Lightweight signals, not pixels

Trace captures the three things you actually need to reconstruct your day — without recording a single frame.

01

Browser history

Trace copies your history from Chrome, Edge, Firefox and other Chromium browsers. No screenshots — just the URL and title metadata your browser already stores, in a unified, full-text searchable store.

02

Active window

Every time you switch applications, Trace records the app name and window title. This lets you search "what app was I in on Tuesday?" without needing any screenshots.

03

Clipboard (opt-in)

If you enable the clipboard collector, Trace saves the text you copy so you can search it later. It's off by default because clipboards often contain secrets. Your call.

None of these three collectors takes screenshots or records video. Screenshot OCR is planned as an optional extra — and when it arrives, it will be off by default and will never leave your machine.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Is there a personal memory app that doesn't record your screen?

Yes. Trace by Fervon captures lightweight signals — browser history, the active app or window, and clipboard text — instead of recording a continuous video. You get a searchable record of everything you did without any screen recording stored.

Why avoid screen recording for personal memory?

Screen recording stores everything: visible passwords, private messages, financial documents. It consumes high CPU and RAM, drains batteries, and often violates IT security policies. It's not the right approach when you just need to remember what you were doing.

What does Trace capture instead?

Trace captures three lightweight signals: (1) browser history from Chrome, Edge and Firefox; (2) the name of the active application or window; and (3) clipboard text when you copy something (opt-in, off by default). No pixels, no video, no screenshots.

How is Trace different from Screenpipe?

Screenpipe records continuous screen video and runs OCR over it. Trace deliberately avoids this: it captures only metadata (URLs, window titles, clipboard snippets) — not pixels. This makes Trace far lighter and safe to use on work machines.

Is Trace safe to use on a work computer?

Trace is designed with this use case in mind. It never records screen video or takes screenshots by default. The exclusion list lets you block specific apps, domains or windows. Everything stays on your machine — nothing is uploaded to any server.
